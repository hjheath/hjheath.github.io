Education

Weather and Climate Models

I studied Physics at New College for four years. I particularly enjoyed writing code to model dynamic systems such as the double pendulum. As a result I chose to do my master’s project in the Atmospheric, Oceanic and Planetary Physics sub-department modelling climate and weather systems.

Together with my supervisor Peter Duben, I worked on improving the efficiency of weather and climate models. We reduced the number of bits used by a computer to represent floating point numbers and measured the precision and accuracy of the resulting forecasts. My results indicated that more than halving the number of bits in the mantissa has little effect on the reliability of forecasts. It is expected that hardware that operates at lower precision will lead to a corresponding reduction in the computational cost of making a forecast.

Gibbs Prize for Public Speaking

I won an Oxford speaking competition for a talk I gave on "The Physics of Bicycles". I tried to answer two questions: why bicycles stay up when you cycle with no hands, and why cyclists in the Tour de France do not pedal when descending.

The answer to the first question is complicated, seemingly obvious answers such as gyroscopic effects have been discredited. Indeed David E. H. Jones constructed and rode with no hands a bicycle with counter spinning wheels to cancel out any gyroscopic effects.

Top cyclists achieve such high speeds when descending that it is more efficient to tuck their body and legs in to reduce drag than it is to pedal as can be seen here.
